Sir Tim Berners-Lee

Inventor of the World Wide Web:
Sir Tim Berners-Lee is credited with inventing the World Wide Web in 1989 while working at CERN (the European Organization for Nuclear Research).
First Website:
He created the first-ever website, which went live on August 6, 1991. The site provided information about the World Wide Web project and explained how to create web pages.
HTML and HTTP:
Berners-Lee developed the fundamental technologies for the web, including HTML (Hypertext Markup Language), HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol), and URLs (Uniform Resource Locators).
World Wide Web Consortium (W3C):
In 1994, he founded the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) to develop open web standards and ensure the long-term growth of the web.
Knighted by Queen Elizabeth II:
In 2004, he was knighted by Queen Elizabeth II for his pioneering work in developing the World Wide Web.
Director of the World Wide Web Foundation:
Berners-Lee is the director of the World Wide Web Foundation, which he founded in 2009 to promote the open web as a public good and a basic right.
ACM Turing Award:
He received the prestigious ACM Turing Award in 2016 for inventing the World Wide Web, the first web browser, and the fundamental protocols and algorithms allowing the web to scale.
Campaigner for Internet Freedom:
Berners-Lee is a vocal advocate for net neutrality and the open web. He has campaigned against internet censorship and the commercialization of the web.
Solid Project:
He is currently working on the Solid project, which aims to decentralize the web by giving users control over their data and fostering a more equitable internet.
Book Author:
Berners-Lee authored a book titled "Weaving the Web: The Original Design and Ultimate Destiny of the World Wide Web" in 1999, where he discusses the history and future potential of the web.
